\section*{Purpose of This Research}

From purely geometric properties of central projection from an $n$-dimensional tangent hyperplane to a hypersphere $S^n(R)$, we construct a unified framework in which multiple independent axes that generate acceleration can coexist without contradiction. We refrain from physical identifications and confine ourselves to geometric propositions and their proofs.

\section*{Proven Geometric Propositions}

\subsection*{Paper [1]: Basic Structure of Central Projection}

\textbf{1. Isomorphism with de Sitter Spacetime}\quad
The induced metric of the Lorentzian central projection coincides numerically with the metric in the Beltrami coordinate system of de Sitter spacetime, establishing complete equivalence as intrinsic geometry. (\S 1.1, Appendix C, Proposition C.1)

\textbf{2. Regularity of Forward and Inverse Projection}\quad
Since $l = \sqrt{R^2 + r^2} \geq R > 0$, the central projection $\Phi$ is $C^\infty$-regular on its entire domain. The inverse $x^\mu = RY^\mu/Y^{n+1}$ is regular on $H^+$. The mapping is bijective. (Appendix B, Proposition B.1)

\textbf{3. Induced Metric and Einstein Tensor}\quad
The pullback metric $g_{\mu\nu} = (R^2/l^2)(\delta_{\mu\nu} - x_\mu x_\nu/l^2)$ is rigorously derived, and the $n$-dimensional Einstein tensor satisfies $G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda_n g_{\mu\nu} = 0$ with $\Lambda_n = (n{-}1)(n{-}2)/(2R^2)$. (\S 3, \S 6, Proposition 6.1)

\textbf{4. Flat Limit}\quad
As $R \to \infty$: $g_{\mu\nu} \to \delta_{\mu\nu}$, $\Lambda \to 0$. The central projection degenerates to the identity map, recovering Minkowski spacetime. (Appendix A)

\subsection*{Paper [2]: Four Geometric Symmetries}

\textbf{5. Symmetry I (Discrete Stability)}\quad
For any coordinate value --- zero, positive integer, or negative integer --- $l_A > 0$ holds and the central projection is globally regular. (\S 3, Theorem 3.1)

\textbf{6. Symmetry II (Axis Equivalence)}\quad
Regardless of which axis in $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$ is chosen as the projection center, the metric, curvature tensor, and Einstein tensor have identical mathematical structure. (\S 4, Theorem 4.1)

\textbf{7. Symmetry III (Geodesic Deviation and Indistinguishability)}\quad
The geodesic deviation $|\xi|^2 = g_{\mu\nu}\xi^\mu\xi^\nu$ is a scalar invariant of dimension $[L^2]$ and the sole means by which an internal observer recognizes curvature. However, neither the sign of coordinates nor the direction of curvature can be distinguished. (\S 5, Theorem 5.2)

\textbf{8. Symmetry IV (Transformability of Subjective Coordinate Systems)}\quad
Subjective coordinate systems centered on different axes are mutually transformable via $T_{A \to B} = \Phi_B^{-1} \circ \Phi_A$, a $C^\infty$ diffeomorphism. The only parameters are the curvature radius $R$ and coordinate values. (\S 6, Theorem 6.1, Proposition 6.5)

\textbf{9. Curvature Radius as Scale Factor}\quad
The Jacobian is $\partial x'^A/\partial x^B = -R^2/(x^B)^2$, with a dimensionless first-order scale factor. The curvature radius $R$ serves as the sole transformation parameter. (\S 6.4, Proposition 6.2)

\subsection*{Paper [3]: Multiple Subjective Spaces and Relativity of Observation}

\textbf{10. Simultaneous Existence of Multiple Subjective Spaces}\quad
From $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$, $n+1$ subjective spaces $\{H_A^+\}$ can be simultaneously constructed. Each possesses structurally identical geometric properties by Symmetry II. (\S 2, Proposition 2.1)

\textbf{11. Limits of Information Obtained by Internal Observers}\quad
An internal observer obtains only quantities from the metric of their own subjective space and cannot access the projection center axis value, absolute position, or the existence of other subjective spaces. (\S 3, Proposition 3.1)

\textbf{12. Exchange of Axis Roles and Relativity of Observation}\quad
What is the ``projection center axis'' for observer $A$ appears as one of the subjective coordinates for observer $B$, and vice versa. No geometric basis exists for determining which standpoint is ``correct.'' (\S 4, Proposition 4.1; \S 5, Theorem 5.1)

\subsection*{Paper [4]: Limits of the Curvature Radius}

\textbf{13. Regularity of the $R \to \infty$ Limit}\quad
As $R \to \infty$, the induced metric asymptotes to flat Euclidean space and $\Lambda = 3/R^2 \to 0$. All terms of the Einstein equation exhibit well-defined asymptotics. (\S 2.1)

\textbf{14. Regularity and Structural Tension at $R \to 0$}\quad
As $R \to 0$, $\Lambda = 3/R^2$ diverges, but all terms remain well-defined for any $R > 0$. The upper bound of geodesic deviation approaches zero while local curvature diverges at order $1/R^2$, giving rise to structural tension. (\S 3.1, \S 3.2)

\textbf{15. Existence of Nested Structure}\quad
For any point $p$ in any subjective space $\Pi_R$, a new subjective space $\Pi_{R'}$ with $p$ as the elevation point exists for any $R' > 0$. (\S 4.2, Proposition 4.1)

\subsection*{Paper [5]: Dimension Addition}

\textbf{16. Two Categories of Dimension Addition}\quad
Type I (under existing curvature radius $R$) and Type II (introducing a new independent curvature radius $R'$). (\S 2.2)

\textbf{17. Dimension Addition to Finite-Curvature Subjective Spaces}\quad
Type I is freely executable. For Type II, unless the new axis and governed dimensions are added simultaneously, the subjective space degenerates to zero dimensions. (\S 4.2, Observation 4.1; \S 4.3, Observations 4.2--4.3)

\subsection*{Paper [6]: Subjective Spaces from Zero to Four Dimensions}

\textbf{18. Characteristics at Each Dimension}\quad
0D: No internal structure. 1D: No direction concept. 2D: Area, angle, geodesic deviation first definable; time axis undetermined. 3D: Volume definable. 4D: Hypervolume definable; number of time axes not uniquely determined. (\S 2--\S 6)

\subsection*{Paper [7]: Continuity of Geodesics}

\textbf{19. Independence of Parent-Child Geodesics}\quad
A geodesic $\gamma$ on $\Pi_R$ and a geodesic $\gamma'$ on child $\Pi_{R'}$ (elevated from a point on $\gamma$) are independently defined geometric objects sharing the same ambient space. (\S 4.3, direct consequence of Theorem 6.1)

\subsection*{Paper [9]: Schwarzschild--de Sitter Exact Solution}

\textbf{20. Unique Construction of the Exact Solution}\quad
The spherically symmetric static exact solution of the vacuum Einstein equation $G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = 0$ ($\Lambda = 3/R^2$) from Paper {[1]} is uniquely determined as the Schwarzschild--de Sitter metric $f(r) = 1 - 2M/r - r^2/R^2$ by Birkhoff's theorem. No additional assumptions required. (\S 3, Eq.\ 3.4)

\textbf{21. Disappearance of Horizon Structure at $R \to 0$}\quad
As $R \to 0$, the cosmological horizon $r_{\mathrm{C}} \to 0$, merging with the black hole horizon and eliminating the observer region. This is the exact-solution-level counterpart of Paper {[4]} \S 3.2. (\S 4.2--\S 4.3)

\textbf{22. Recovery of Horizon Structure in Child Subjective Space}\quad
By Proposition 4.1, a child subjective space $\Pi_{R'}$ with independent $R'$ can be constructed, and the inter-horizon observer region recovers when $R'$ is finite. (\S 5.2--\S 5.3)

\subsection*{Paper [10]: Dimensional Interpretation of the $R \to 0$ Limit}

\textbf{23. Correspondence between $R \to 0$ and Zero-Dimensional Subjective Space}\quad
The $R \to 0$ limit corresponds geometrically to Paper {[6]}'s zero-dimensional subjective space: disappearance of geodesic deviation, internal structure, and curvature recognition. (\S 3, Observation 3.1)

\textbf{24. Description as Dimensional Transition}\quad
Stage 1 (Dimensional collapse): parent's dimensional structure is lost, approaching zero dimensions. Stage 2 (Dimensional reconstruction): child subjective space is elevated via Proposition 4.1 and dimensional structure unfolds. The geodesic ``terminus'' is a transition point, not annihilation. (\S 5, Observation 5.1)

\subsection*{Paper [8]: Singularity of Four Dimensions}

\textbf{25. Integer Property of the Circumscribed Hypersphere Diameter}\quad
The diameter $2\sqrt{n}$ of the circumscribed hypersphere of a unit $n$-dimensional hypercube is an integer iff $n$ is a perfect square. Excluding trivial $n = 1$, $n = 4$ is the smallest non-trivial dimension satisfying this. (\S 3)

\textbf{26. Settling at Four Dimensions}\quad
The dimension simultaneously satisfying symmetry preservation, integer property, and minimization conditions is $n = 4$. (\S 12)

\textbf{27. Origin of the Time Axis}\quad
An odd number of axis inversions is required for negative volume. The most economical single-axis inversion generates $(-1)^1 = -1$. This ``1-to-3 asymmetry'' gives rise to one time axis and three spatial axes in $(-,+,+,+)$. (\S 13)

\section*{What This Research Does Not Claim}

This research is confined to purely geometric propositions and their proofs. The following claims are not made:

\begin{itemize}
  \item That internal observers of subjective spaces physically exist
  \item That ``relativity of observation'' corresponds to any specific theory of relativity in physics
  \item That a particular subjective space corresponds to our universe
  \item That physical laws are derivable from the structure of subjective spaces
\end{itemize}

These physical interpretations require additional assumptions beyond the geometric propositions of this research (Paper [3], \S 7).
