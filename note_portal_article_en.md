# From the Necessity of 5 Dimensions to 62 Particles and Four Forces — A Guide to All 36 Papers on the Central Projection Framework

## Purpose of This Article

This article is a **comprehensive guide** to a series of studies that, starting solely from the geometric properties of central projection, arrive at a classification of the 62 particles appearing in the Standard Model of particle physics and a unified description of the four fundamental forces.

All 36 papers are published as preprints on Zenodo (PDF, LaTeX, and Markdown in both Japanese and English). Supplement 4 has been retracted.

## Main Claims

This research makes three claims.

**1. Number-theoretic necessity of a 5-dimensional background space**
By Lagrange's four-square theorem and the projective-geometric conditions of central projection, full-sphere coverage in discrete space requires exactly 5 dimensions (W5). Our "3 spatial + 1 temporal" dimensions are derived from number theory.

**2. Combinatorial derivation of 62 Standard Model states**
From a 6-dimensional structure — a 5D hypercube augmented with an 8-valued discrete label axis Q — 62 states are derived by pure enumeration, without using any physics terminology (W8). These 62 states have a structure that can be mapped to the 61 Standard Model particles + the graviton.

**3. Unified description of the four forces**
Through mode classification of shape-invariant waves (W10) and their interactions (W11), the four forces — electromagnetism, the weak force, the strong force, and gravity — are uniformly described as the same operation, "axial displacement transfer," applied to different axes.

---

## What Is Not Claimed

- That the Standard Model has been physically "derived"
- Derivation of specific numerical values of masses, coupling constants, or mixing matrices
- Prediction of the existence of undiscovered particles
- Rigorous reconstruction of gauge groups in the continuum limit

The correspondence with the Standard Model in W8 §9 is "one possible interpretation" and is not a claim of the paper.

## Comparison with Existing Theories

How this framework differs from existing theoretical approaches.

**Difference from string theory**: String theory requires 10 (or 11) dimensions and suffers from numerous degrees of freedom in extra-dimension compactification (the landscape problem). In this framework, 5 dimensions are uniquely determined by number theory, and no extra dimensions exist.

**Difference from Grand Unified Theories (GUTs)**: GUTs unify forces by enlarging gauge groups (SU(5), SO(10), etc.), but the choice of group itself is an external assumption. In this framework, particle classification is derived from the combinatorial structure of hypercubes, and gauge groups are identified post hoc.

**Relationship to the Standard Model**: The Standard Model contains 19 free parameters. The main external assumptions of this framework are only the number of dimensions (5) and the number of Q-axis bits (3), deriving 62 states from orders-of-magnitude fewer structural inputs.

## Open Problems

**Quantitative derivation of mass values**: The geometric origin of mass has been shown qualitatively (W11, Supplement 3, mass discussion), but derivation of specific numerical values remains unachieved.

**Internal derivation of the Q-axis 8 values**: The reason the Q-axis has 2³=8 states is an external assumption based on consistency with the Standard Model; why not 2²=4 or 2⁴=16 has not been derived (W8 §1.1).

**Rigorous reconstruction of gauge groups**: The rigorous transition from discrete combinatorial structures to continuous gauge groups SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1) is incomplete.
